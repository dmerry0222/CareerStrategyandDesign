How different students move through the four-stage CS&D continuum — what each already brings, where each has real gaps, and how the behavioral outcomes do work at the two moments that matter most: securing work-based learning, and landing what comes after Suffolk.

On the question of students who “already know this.” Several of the personas below arrive with genuine proficiency in something CS&D covers. That is expected, and it is not an argument against the requirement.

We routinely teach students things some of them already know — that is what a shared curriculum is. The relevant question is not whether a student has seen the content before, but whether the structure moves them from where they actually are. A student who arrives with a good resume does not repeat the resume; they hit Stage 2’s skills-based translation, then Stage 3’s outcomes-not-duties revision, then Stage 4’s alignment to a named target. The artifact is the same word. The demand is not.

Each persona’s first list is what they brought. The second, longer list is what the four stages actually did.
